1: Screening of antimicrobial peptides from bacillus spp and its activity against multidrug resistant Escherichia coli

Research on the screening of AMPs from Bacillus spp. has primarily focused on identifying and characterizing these molecules in order to understand their potential as new classes of antibiotics. One common method for screening AMPs is to use a bacterial assay, in which the AMP is added to a culture of a target pathogen, such as Escherichia coli, and the ability of the AMP to inhibit growth of the pathogen is measured. One area of research on AMPs from Bacillus spp. has been their activity against multidrug-resistant (MDR) E. coli. MDR strains of E. coli are a significant public health concern because they are resistant to many commonly used antibiotics, making them difficult to treat. Studies have shown that AMPs from Bacillus spp. have strong activity against MDR E. coli, and may have the potential to be developed as new treatments for infections caused by these resistant strains. Some specific examples of Bacillus spp. derived AMPs include: -Bac7c, a peptide derived from Bacillus subtilis which is able to inhibit the growth of both MDR and susceptible E. coli -Bac9, also a peptide derived from Bacillus subtilis it is also able to inhibit the growth of MDR E.coli.
